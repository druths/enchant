Remote visualization made simple

A lot of data science happens on remote servers. This makes visualization a pain. How can we easily capture the plots, diagrams, and output of processes - regardless of where they are run?

enchant provides a simple, lightweight solution. Imagine a virtual space where you could easily push any and all output - whether it's images, plots, text, or arbitrary HTML. That's enchant.

How it works

The workspace. Your enchant workspace consists of notebooks. You have only one workspace, but you can have as many notebooks as you want.

The notebook. A notebook is a log of results. Each result is called a block. Every block has a title and a timestamp. Put whatever you want into your notebook - text, images, HTML. Once it's in, it stays there. So you can always go back through your log and find results that you generated five minutes ago, yesterday, or last January.

Posting results. You post new blocks to a notebook using either UNIX simple command line programs or programmatic functions:

Command line

$ enchant img ineq_nb \
  "Inequality by decade" \
  ineq_dec.png

Python

send_text('inequality_nb',
          'Basic stats',
          '\n'.join(stats))
Current support for UNIX and python are found in the enchant-client project.

How it compares

enchant vs. files. If you wanted, you could scp images and plots from your compute server whenever you generate a new result. But that's a pain - it's slow ... and it doesn't play well with long-running jobs. With enchant, you can check up on your results from anywhere right on your smartphone!

enchant vs. jupyter. Jupyter and Zepplin are awesome interactive notebook. But they have a weird state problem: it's hard to know what version of the code in the notebook generated the plot you're looking at. Admittedly, enchant doesn't specifically bind results to code. But by decoupling the result log from the code, it becomes less confusing.

enchant vs. a laptop. Sure... you can run all your tasks on your laptop. But many projects will outgrow a desktop - and when they do, research results get fragmented.
